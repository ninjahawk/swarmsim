2026-06-01 2.50 Opened the co-adaptation thread: the first experiment in which a behavioral trait is heritable and under selection rather than fixed by hand (evolution/escape_evolution.py). Each prey carries its own collective-escape weight w (the F70 trait: a force w times the shared unit vector fleeing the predator centroid), and w is inherited. Predators are the hardest found, predictive encirclement at lead 2 (F66), six of them, slow-prey regime, N=150. I chose a capture/removal fitness model deliberately (over proximity-survival and energy-budget alternatives): an agent within a small kill radius of a predator is captured at a fixed hazard rate and replaced by a mutated clone of a random survivor, holding population size fixed. The question is the evolutionary form of the F70 valley -- since a weak escape is worse than none and only w at or above the alignment strength outruns the trap, does selection drive a cautious population across the valley to the escape regime or trap it? Built the experiment on a new vectorized predator-prey harness (vectorized_predator.py + vectorized_predator_prey.py) that I verified bit-identical to the legacy F66/F70 scripts. Result: over 150 tu the outcome is set sharply by the starting weight -- a population seeded in the escape regime is stable and almost never caught (start at w=2, stays at 2.0, about 6 captures, order parameter 1.0), while one seeded at or below the valley stalls far short of escape (start at 0, rises only to about 0.27, order parameter 0.22, 1804 captures) and the capture toll peaks precisely in the valley. A 400-tu run separates a barrier from a brake: selection on w is directional-upward from every start, but the valley throttles the climb so hard that from no escape the mean weight crawls to only about 0.5 in 400 tu and never reaches the escape threshold, while a start past the worst of the valley climbs faster and accelerates. The F70 force-versus-alignment threshold therefore has a population-genetic image: a strong evolutionary brake, not an absolute barrier -- escape behavior is easy to keep but very hard to evolve from scratch, because the path runs through a region where partial commitment is actively selected against (strong evolutionary hysteresis / a first-mover problem). This is the domination-not-blending theme (F16/F24/F70) read at the evolutionary level, and it inverts the naive reading of F70 that escape wins -- escape wins only where it is already present. 2026-05-31 1.25 Self-test combining the steering-bandwidth and many-wrongs results: does a noisy crowd track a MOVING goal, and does the per-agent noise cost steering bandwidth (collective/moving_goal_crowd.py, 8 seeds)? The goal direction rotates at a rate omega and every agent biases toward the current goal carrying its own fixed angular offset. I committed in advance to a prediction -- that the many-wrongs average, being spatial and instantaneous, would add no lag, so noise would lower the bandwidth only by shrinking the averaged-bias magnitude, which falls as the exponential of minus half the squared per-agent spread, so the bandwidth should scale with that factor. The prediction is wrong, instructively. For per-agent spreads up to a radian the tracking-accuracy-versus-turning-rate curves lie almost on top of one another and the bandwidth is unchanged (about 0.20 radians per time unit) even though the magnitude factor has fallen to six tenths -- a noisy crowd turns to follow the goal as well as a sharp leader does. The reason the magnitude reduction does not cost bandwidth is that the offsets are static and rotate with the goal, so the averaged bias points cleanly at the current goal each step and adds no lag, while the reduced magnitude still leaves the pull above the threshold needed to steer; the bandwidth is set by the response time, not the pull magnitude, in this range. The magnitude law bites only when it pushes the pull below threshold, which is exactly the noise ceiling: at a spread of one and a half radians, past the ceiling found earlier, tracking does not degrade gracefully but collapses, the static case itself becoming unreliable with large run-to-run scatter and the fastest turn essentially untracked. Noise thus has a binary effect on moving-goal tracking, free below the ceiling and catastrophic above it, rather than a graded bandwidth cost.
