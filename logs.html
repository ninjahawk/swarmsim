2026-06-17 5.50 Returned to the sandpile chapter to pursue one genuine open question in depth rather than moving on: is the slope sandpile simple-FSS or multifractal, the moment-spectrum test that is the field's real universality discriminator. Built and self-tested the moment-scaling machinery, validated it by reproducing the known result that the canonical BTW model is multifractal in toppling number but near-FSS in area (Finding S11), then applied it to the slope model. Found its avalanche footprints are filamentary, area growing only linearly with system size, while the total activity is space-filling, so each footprint bond topples about L times. The scaling is anomalous, not in the stochastic Manna class, and geometrically distinct from BTW, a caveat-free upgrade of the earlier exponent-based universality result. Required adding an avalanche-area counter to the fast engine (validated bit-for-bit) and fixing a slow-equilibration subtlety in the 2-D pile (Finding S12).
2026-06-18 4.50 Turned yesterday's filamentary-avalanche snapshot into a mechanism and tested it. Using conditional averages, a cleaner instrument than the moment spectra because they ignore the system-size cutoff that the giant avalanches otherwise dominate, I asked whether a single length scale governs each 2-D slope avalanche. The spatial half holds cleanly: energy tracks size one to one, and a footprint of a given area carries a number of topplings growing as the area to the power 1.93, close to the predicted square, confirming the thin front that sweeps its bonds about as many times as it is long. But the consistency conditions do not all close: routed through duration the size-area exponent comes out 1.79 against 1.93 measured directly, a real gap, because some avalanches last long by lingering rather than reaching farther. Single-scale scaling holds in space and loosens in time, the 2-D residue of the 1-D avalanche families found at the start of the chapter. The energy-duration plane is the 2-D counterpart of the book's avalanche wedge and hugs its steeper boundary (Finding S13).
2026-06-19 4.50 Recorded and measured the geometry of individual two-dimensional slope avalanches. The footprint is a one-bond-wide ballistic filament with a mass-radius dimension of one, thinner than the exactly solvable directed sandpile and far from the compact original, which I ran through the same pipeline as a control. This is the geometric reason the footprint dimension came out near one, now read off real avalanche shapes rather than an exponent (Finding S14).
2026-06-20 4.50 Tested whether the one-bond-wide avalanche filament is caused by the deterministic redistribution rule or merely incidental, by adding a tunable stochastic split that diverts a fraction of each topple's downhill sand sideways to a random neighbour, interpolating toward the Manna class. Validated that the knob leaves the original dynamics bit-identical at zero and conserves sand. As the split turns on, the footprint dimension climbs from one to 1.87, with two lattice sizes agreeing so the effect is intrinsic, and passing the directed sandpile's value of three-halves near a small setting: the filament is specific to the deterministic rule. But the avalanche statistics do not become Manna-like, the moment drift stays anomalous, so the model compactifies in shape without changing universality class (Finding S15).
2026-06-22 5.00 Asked whether the two-dimensional avalanche filament is genuinely a one-dimensional object by measuring the real one-dimensional avalanche with the same instruments. Added the footprint recorder to the one-dimensional engine, which had never had it, and validated it bit-for-bit against a full lattice scan. Across lattices from 512 to 4096 nodes the one-dimensional avalanche is a solid, gap-free interval of mass-radius dimension one (a Cantor self-test confirms the estimator would catch a fractal), laid down by a perfectly ballistic front whose topple time equals distance from the launch node. Its conditional exponents match the two-dimensional values, so the two-dimensional filament is a one-dimensional avalanche embedded in the plane, and dimension near one is intrinsic to a slope avalanche rather than an artifact of measuring it in two dimensions. The duration-scaling gap found in two dimensions is present in one dimension too, inherited from the line and wedge avalanche families found at the start of the chapter, so it is a real property of the rule, not a two-dimensional accident. Only directedness changes with dimension. Also broke the equilibration ceiling that had capped the work at 256 a side, reaching a verified stationary 512-node state in about 25 seconds, and confirmed the slow rise of the angle of repose with size is a real finite-size effect (Finding S16).
2026-06-23 4.50 Settled the week-old duration question using yesterday's larger lattices. Measured the single-scale over-determination gap across lattices from 96 to 512 a side, each at a verified steady state. The gap does not heal: the spatial relations reach their exact single-scale values as the lattice grows (size as area squared, area in proportion to duration), but the size-duration slope stalls at about 1.75 rather than two, so the gap grows with size toward a true residual of about a fifth, larger than the one-dimensional anchor of 0.11 from yesterday. Single-scale scaling is exact in space and permanently residual in time, so area, not duration, is the clean scaling variable. The one-dimensional cross-check confirms the anomaly is intrinsic and not a two-dimensional artifact, and it is stronger in two dimensions, where a front has more ways to linger. Closes the scaling-theory arc that began with the moment-spectrum work (Finding S17).
2026-06-25 4.00 Closed the last open question of the scaling-theory arc: whether the avalanche-area multifractality I found weeks ago is a real asymptotic feature or a finite-size artifact. Gathered the area of every avalanche across seven lattice sizes from 64 to 512 a side, each at a verified steady state, and localized the multifractal drift in a sliding window of sizes so it would fade if it were finite-size. It does not fade. The drift holds near 0.2 across a fourfold range of sizes and extrapolates to 0.17, well clear of zero, where a finite-size artifact would have fallen by a factor of four. So the multifractality is asymptotic. This falsifies the naive prediction from the spatial single-scale result, but reconciles with it: that result governs the typical avalanche while the moment spectrum is set by the rare largest ones, so the model is single-scale in its averages and multifractal in its tails at once. The effective area dimension rises from 1.14 at the low moments to 1.34 at the high ones, showing the mechanism directly (Finding S18).
2026-06-28 2.50 Tested the one loose end I left flagged three days ago, that the typical avalanche footprint might be fattening at the largest lattices rather than staying the thin one-bond filament I had measured all month. Re-ran the footprint geometry at four sizes from 192 to 512 a side with ten to twelve settled states each, double what I had before, and split every lattice's avalanches into the typical bulk and the rare tail to ask the fattening question of each separately. It is the tail, not the typical avalanche: the typical footprint's median area grows in step with the lattice side, its shape dimension stays pinned at one across all sizes, and its footprints stay one bond wide out to 512 a side, while only the rare largest avalanches grow. The mean-area growth slope that had read 1.22 at five seeds falls to 0.99 once averaged over ten, so that number was the scatter of a few giant avalanches in a thin tail, not a real exponent. The filament does not fatten. The established geometric picture stands, sharpened rather than overturned, with the growth I half-saw belonging entirely to the multifractal tail I had already found (Finding S19).
2026-06-30 1.00 Settled a small open question from two weeks ago: whether the angle of repose has a well-defined infinite-volume limit or keeps growing slowly with lattice size. Fit two competing models to the seven plateau values collected in June and compared them by AIC. The logarithmic model fits clearly better than the 1/L saturation model (DAIC = 9.7, R-squared 0.990 vs 0.960), and the per-doubling increments are roughly constant rather than halving, as the 1/L form would require. The repose most likely diverges logarithmically rather than approaching a finite limit (Finding S20).
