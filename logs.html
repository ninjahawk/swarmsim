2026-05-26 1.50 Asked whether an informed minority can steer a turning flock, not just hold a fixed bearing (collective/moving_goal.py). The goal direction rotates at angular velocity omega and the informed minority always biases toward the current goal; the question is how well the flock tracks a moving target, how far it lags, and whether there is a critical turning rate above which tracking collapses. Result: the flock has a finite steering bandwidth. At ten percent informed it tracks a stationary goal nearly perfectly, lags a slow turn by tens of degrees, and fails entirely once the turning rate reaches about a tenth of a radian per time unit, where the goal out-runs the heading by more than ninety degrees; above that the rotating bias time-averages to nothing and the flock ignores it. The bandwidth scales with the informed fraction in step with the response time measured earlier -- doubling the informed fraction roughly doubles the critical turning rate, and both match the inverse of the settle time (about a ninth of a radian per time unit at ten percent informed, about a fifth at twenty). The time-domain response time and the frequency-domain steering bandwidth are the same timescale, the rate at which the minority can re-aim the bulk; below it the flock behaves as a low-pass steering filter, passing slow turns with a lag and attenuating fast ones. A new cost appears that fixed-goal steering never had: forcing a turn faster than the flock can follow drops the order parameter to 0.78, the lowest in the whole leadership thread, because the leaders pull in a direction the bulk cannot track and tear the flock. Steering within the bandwidth is free, steering beyond it is paid for in coherence -- the same tension that governs predation, where redirecting a flock costs coherence exactly when it outpaces what alignment can propagate. 2026-05-26 1.25 Tested whether leadership is a signal or an identity (collective/rotate_leaders.py). The leadership experiments all used a fixed informed set, in contrast to the contagion thread, where slow-recoverer vaccination fails once the per-agent recovery-rate label drifts, because there the exploitable thing is a durable per-agent identity. Leadership should be the opposite kind of signal -- the flock follows a shared direction, not particular individuals -- so rotating which agents are informed, while keeping the goal direction fixed, should not hurt, because the total injected directed force is unchanged regardless of who carries it. Held the informed fraction and goal fixed but re-drew which agents are informed every tau time units, sweeping tau from very fast to never. Result: rotation never hurts -- accuracy equals or exceeds the fixed-leader baseline at every rotation period -- and faster rotation actively improves steering while sharply reducing seed variance (at five percent informed, accuracy rises from 0.86 to 0.94 as the cross-seed spread halves; at ten percent, from 0.97 to 0.99 with the spread falling fivefold). Fast rotation smears the same total pull uniformly over all agents, and a weak uniform bias on everyone steers more reliably than a strong bias concentrated on a fixed subset that must propagate through alignment. This is the exact opposite of the drifting-label result in the contagion thread: contagion targeting needs a durable per-agent identity, while leadership needs only a durable shared direction, so turning over the individuals who carry it is harmless or helpful. It closes the question of what makes a collective-control target exploitable -- fragile if it rests on a persistent per-agent label, robust if it rests on a shared global quantity -- and nuances the earlier spatial-vaccination null: distributing a control helps when injecting a directional force but is irrelevant when removing nodes. Result: for small conflict (up to ~90 degrees) the flock COMPROMISES, traveling almost exactly the midpoint direction with a tight cross-seed spread; past a critical angle of roughly 90-120 degrees it switches to CONSENSUS, where the cross-seed heading spread explodes (22 to 74 degrees) because different seeds commit to one goal or the other -- averaging two near-opposed directions is not a viable heading, so the symmetry breaks. The flock picks one direction rather than splitting (order parameter stays above 0.96, smaller-cluster fraction below 0.16 even at direct opposition). And the majority wins: at 180-degree opposition, a 21-to-14 informed-agent margin already biases the flock +0.42 toward the majority goal, rising to +0.93 as the margin grows, with the seed spread shrinking and splitting vanishing -- group direction is an effective majority vote among the informed. The deeper point is that the alignment force does not merely propagate one shared direction (F72) but ARBITRATES among several: average-when-compatible, vote-when-not, the same domination-not-blending physics seen whenever competing global drives meet in this model. 2026-05-26 1.25 Opened a collective-decision-making thread (leadership.py): can a small informed minority steer the whole flock? Following Couzin et al. (2005), a fraction rho of agents carry a preferred travel direction and feel an extra bias force; the rest are naive followers with no goal knowledge. Swept rho from 0 to 0.5 at two leader strengths, measuring directional accuracy (cosine of the mean flock velocity to the goal) and cohesion. Just 5% informed (18 of 350 agents) already steers the flock to accuracy 0.63-0.83; 10% reaches 0.87-0.96; accuracy saturates near unity by 20%. Stronger leaders reach a given accuracy at smaller rho and with lower seed-to-seed variance. Cohesion is never lost (order parameter stays above 0.995 everywhere) -- steering is cohesion-free, the opposite of the predator case where redirecting the flock costs coherence. The mechanism is that every leader shares one common direction vector, which alignment amplifies to the naive majority: the constructive case of the shared-signal principle from the escape results (F70/F71). Leadership, committed collective escape, and flock formation are one phenomenon -- a globally shared direction is amplified, a locally heterogeneous one is averaged away.
